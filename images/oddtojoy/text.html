O D D   T O   J O Y

The final movement of Beethoven’s 9th Symphony is the official anthem of the European Union.

It is a proud, euphoric piece of classical music with a long history of usage by governments, regimes, political groups etc. as an affirmation of beliefs and ideology.

Originally set to Friedrich Schiller’s poem Ode to Joy, from where it has gotten its name, the composition has been stated by the European Council as being the anthem not just of the EU, but of Europe as a whole.

In order to not relate to any one nation specifically, the tune has to relate to noone at all. And yet the people of the EU nations feel an emotional connection to it whenever and whereever it is played. They feel somehow represented.

“It’s, you know, that gut feeling that we feel when we experience something pathetic and we say ‘Oh my god, I am so moved, this is SO deep’. But you never know what this depth is. It’s a void.” – Slavoj Zizek

The following case studies show a selection of amateur musicians performing “Ode to Joy” in their own way; at their own pace; expressing themselves privately, enthusiastically, and showing the world how they are getting on.

Some are ready to teach the world how to play; others are doing really badly and are asking for help; others again are treating the whole tune as a bad joke.

But in these case studies, all of these musicians are suddenly forced to play together – involuntarily and without any prior warning or time for preparation.

Some manage to sync up pretty well and harmonise to a certain degree; others fail completely and are both out of sync and out of tune. And some are not even trying.
